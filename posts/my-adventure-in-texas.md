Never in my life would I have thought I'd have any direct involvement with Texas, but that's life, right? You never actually know what will happen.

But it turns out it wasn't an accident. I decided to challenge the status quo (as my former job's culture would say) and try to get into one of the greatest Computer Science universities in the US: The University of Texas at Austin.

Of course I won't be moving to Texas — I have my life here in Brazil. That's why I decided to go with the online program, and it turns out it's perfect: I'll receive a high-quality education while working a full-time job.

Here's a little about how the process went until I received my offer.

## The Decision

I chose UT Austin for two reasons. The first is that it's one of the top 10 CS universities in the US. The second is that they offer the program at a price I can actually afford while working.

I also considered the Georgia Tech program and the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign, which were also affordable and highly ranked, but the UT Austin program had the perfect balance of updated courses and a strong community.

## The Process

The requirements to apply are actually quite simple — you have to send them the following:

- Statement of Purpose
- CV/Resume
- Letters of Recommendation (optional)
- Transcripts
- TOEFL/IELTS (for international students like me)
- GRE (optional)

There's also an application fee.

For me, as an international student, the hardest parts were the Transcripts — which have to be translated by an official translator — and the TOEFL scores.

I personally didn't submit a recommendation letter, but that's because I was confident that my CV and experience in the field would be enough for them to see that I'm capable of completing the program.

You can find more details about the application here: https://cdso.utexas.edu/apply (including other related programs).

## After Being Accepted

When I received the news I was very happy, but after reading the full email I realized I had to send my transcripts and diploma again — by mail, from Brazil to Texas (there are other options, but my undergraduate university didn't have any of them; you can check them here: https://gradschool.utexas.edu/admissions/decision).

In the end I was able to send just the online version of my documents, because Brazilian federal universities don't provide physical diplomas. The bureaucracy took a few weeks and several emails, but eventually everything was resolved.

## Conclusion

That's it. I'll probably start classes after July and I'm very excited about it. I hope I can enjoy this journey and finally become a Master.
